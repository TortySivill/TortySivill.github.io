Can we explain what we don’t understand?


Exploring the relationship between knowledge, understanding, explanations and how they relate to explainable artificial intelligence.


The pleasure of being a student in your 20s can be encapsulated by the experience of renting a room in a house you share with complete strangers. The other day I splurged on some bottled Asahis - the best beer in the world - with every intention of enjoying them in the sunshine. Unfortunately, the following afternoon I realised they’d been ‘borrowed’. This experience rather painfully brought to mind the work of Birmingham philosopher Scott Sturgeon on the Gettier Problem:

Suppose I burgle your house, find two bottles of Newcastle Brown in the kitchen, drink and replace them. You remember purchasing the ale and come to believe there will be two bottles waiting for you at home. Your belief is justified and true, but you do not know what’s going on.

The thought experiment exemplifies how difficult it is for humans to understand knowledge and how it relates to our own beliefs. Epistemology is the branch of philosophy that deals with exactly this kind of problem, studying the theory of knowledge. It’s considered one of the four main branches of philosophy and can be traced back to Ancient Greece with the likes of Aristotle, Hume, and Descartes all having thrown their hat into the epistemological ring.

So many people think I’m joking when I say that I regularly reference Aristotle in my writing. In an industry that moves as quickly as machine learning, you’re mad if you reference anything older than 5 years. Epistemologists, however, focus on identifying potential sources of knowledge such as perception, reason and memory. Where have we heard those words before in the machine learning literature? Everywhere! These ideas are the backbone of artificial intelligence, they have spawned deep learning, neural networks, logic programming. So, as I boldly construct a PhD research proposal that involves ‘extracting knowledge from data” I think it's important to have these conversations running in the background.

Knowledge vs. Understanding

The first idea I want to focus on is the difference between knowledge and understanding. Semantically, knowledge seems to be more concerned with universal truth and facts whereas understanding seems to be more subjective and individual. There is of course much ambiguity within the epistemological literature but John Locke, a 17th century English philosopher summarised the definition nicely:

Understanding can set down any Measure of the Certainty of our Knowledge

Alternatively, understanding requires us to define which parts of our accumulated knowledge we believe to be true. In fact, Kvanvig claims that epistemologists should focus on understanding rather than knowledge. He argues that an understanding of a subject is incompatible with false “central” knowledge. The use of central in this definition allows for the argument that an understanding of a subject may still be false. So, if an understanding reaches a deeper level than knowledge, where do explanations fit within this model?

Explaining vs Understanding

It has long been acknowledged that understanding and explanations have a complex bi-directional relationship. To exemplify this, consider explaining a really complicated maths problem to a friend. For me, this is the Monty Hall problem - I’ve lost track of the amount of time I’ve spent agonising over that one. Explaining a problem requires you to understand it to a certain level. Think about the times you have started explaining something until you realise that you don’t understand it very well yourself. This view is discussed by Keil et al. (Keil 2004):

Explanations may highlight incompleteness. An explainer will often encounter gaps in understanding that may remain largely invisible when in the form of intuitive theories. Thus, the process of trying to explain explicitly a system to another, or even to oneself, often brings the incompleteness of one’s understanding into much harsher relief

Similarly, explaining something to a friend often deepens your own understanding of that problem. 20th century German philosopher, Hempel’s Explanation-Understanding Condition formalises these ideas:

E is an explanation of some fact F only if someone who possesses E understands F—at least in normal circumstances.

So to what extent should we understand something before we start to explain it? Putting this into the context of explainable AI (XAI), specifically explainable machine learning, starts to uncover some interesting questions. If we view machine learning as a pipeline that starts with some data, applies some machine learning technique and delivers a result, should we view explanations holistically or hone in on individual parts of the pipeline. Most modern research in XAI focuses on the latter parts of that pipeline, either explaining the machine learning technique or explaining the output. There is surprisingly little research conducted in explaining the data. Does it make sense to explain the final output of the pipeline without explaining the processes at the beginning?

Further questions arise when we relate explainability back to understanding. Do all of the explainable AI techniques in the literature understand the underlying process well enough to deliver an effective explanation? Furthermore, if we are providing explanations that are built on insufficient understanding, how can we trust in these explanations? David Hume, an 18th century philosopher, exemplifies the dangers of this:

From causes which appear similar we expect similar effects. This is the sum of all our experimental conclusions. Now it seems evident that, if this conclusion were formed by reason, it would be as perfect at first, and upon one instance, as after ever so long a course of experience. But the case is far otherwise

A false explanation of a machine learning process may result in us changing our own understanding to justify the explanation. Moreover, many of the XAI techniques in the literature zoom-in to provide specific explanations for a specific attribute of the machine learning pipeline. Can an explanation of a subset of the process still be valid in the context of the understanding of the entire system? Through my research, I’m trying to think more deeply about these questions, focusing on building an understanding of the data at the beginning of the machine learning pipeline, exploring what constitutes an explanation for data and how we apply this understanding to explanations downstream.

Explaining vs Describing

I believe a reason that we don’t see a large amount of research for explaining data is that many believe we already have a large number of techniques for this, commonly known as summary statistics. When first given a dataset, there are certain observables I may observe to be true such as the shape, spread, noise. I would argue that this knowledge does not reflect an understanding of the data and therefore should not constitute an effective explanation of the data. They are more aligned with a description of the data.

Michael Novak, an American philosopher from the 20th century defines the differences between explaining and describing as:

Description means to “give an account of” and explanation means “to make clear a cause or reason”.

This definition of explainability coincides with Aristotle’s definition that to explain something is to provide the cause for something. Perhaps, moving forwards we can apply this idea to data and equate an explanation of the data as relaying the causes of the data. Representation learning, a huge body of work within machine learning attempts to learn semantically meaningful factors generating high dimensional datasets and more recently, the literature has started to consider how to extract the causal mechanisms behind complex data. Whether learning representations alone is enough to constitute an explanation of the data is an open question, however it is clear to see they aid our understanding of the mechanisms behind the data.

To conclude, consider the statement by 20th century philosopher, Gaston Bachelard:

We try to explain the flower by the fertiliser

XAI techniques are becoming increasingly complex as the pressure to contribute to this space grows. We are not far off needing explanations for the explanations themselves. Perhaps the best thing those working on XAI can do right now is take a step back and ask whether explanations make sense in the context of their own understanding of a system.
