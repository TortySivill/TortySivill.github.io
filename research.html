Towards Ethical Moral Machines

I am passionate about considering the ways in which technology impacts society. Particularly interesting to me is the ethical implications of modern day artificial intelligence. There are many emerging ethical issues surrounding artificial intelligence today. These include accountability, transparency, and bias of systems. However, this project is primarily concerned with the use of intelligent systems that are being used to make ethical decisions. One such venture is the domain of autonomous vehicles where the significance of this ethical problem was highlighted recently in Arizona, where a driverless car ran down a pedestrian. This tragedy highlights the necessity to explore and question how these autonomous vehicles make difficult decisions such that on their inevitable occurrence we can be assured that an intelligent system acts in the most ethical way.

My Masters thesis investigates state of the art in building computational models of moral decision making and how they may be used in the future to automate ethical decision making. The thesis was submitted in May 2018 and received an overall mark of 90%, winning the Bristol Computer Science Department award for the best Masters Research Project. My primary research hypothesis is:

Is it possible to computationally model human moral decision making and can these models of morality help to achieve the overall goal of automating ethical decision making?

Answering these questions begins with examining the state of the art in computational models for learning human morality which we evaluate on an original dataset to question its generality. We then propose the use of Monte Carlo algorithms such that point estimates are generated more robustly than previous studies. We then dissect and question the assumptions of human morality, made by state of the art, proposing adjustments to the model. Finally, we question the use of aggregation methods to combine individual moral preferences into an overall model for ethical decision making and extend the model beyond the trolley problem. The focus of this dissertation is to raise a plethora of questions and issues, surrounding current perspectives in the field of ethical artificial intelligence, that must be addressed. However, the main achievements of this project can be summarised below:

  • Researched and replicated state of the art in computational models of ethics using the PyMC3 framework and Markov Chain Monte Carlo algorithms. This involved the use of convergence heuristics to ensure model was specified most effectively.

  • Challenged the assumptions made by state of the art to propose a parametrisation founded in mathematical theory. This adaptation to the state of the art resulted in a 2.5\% increase in predictive model accuracy.

  • Challenged the assumption made by state of the art to propose a novel parametrisation based in philosophical theory. This adaptation raised important questions surrounding utility and moral theory and found evidence of a correlation between two central ethical perspectives.

  • Challenged the state of the art by extending idea of population subdivision to achieve 63.6\% accuracy when using learned individual morals to predict gender. Although most importantly, raised interesting questions surrounding the impact of unfounded specific assumptions.

  • Implemented state of the art in computational social choice to question whether models of morality could be used to automate ethical decision making. Implemented an aggregated model that achieves 78\% predictive accuracy over moral decisions.

  • First, to our knowledge, to test a model of moral decision making over multiple domains. Implemented model achieves an accuracy of 68% over more diverse, complex moral dilemmas.
