Overview

When I started my PhD I was really interested ethical AI and through exploring the intersection between people and technology I began to focus my work on interpretable AI. Despite it becoming a buzzword in the past few years, there is still so much work to be done in this space: even figuring out what we really mean by explainable . I am particularly interested in bridging the disconnect between computer science and the social sciences in interpretable AI.

The past year has also demonstrated an urgent need for research within health based AI. AI has proven it has massive potential, but there is no point in having all these fancy methods if they cant be used in practice. We have also seen an explosion of biomedical data that is highly complex, describing systems along multiple dimensions. My research explores the ways in which we can harness this data through AI systems that are inherently interpretable and useful to the humans using them.

II
March 2020 - Present
Interpretable AI for Health Related Time Series Data

Time series data in health has the potential to unlock insight into biological processes, the effectiveness of treatments and the progression of disease. Despite this, relatively little progress has been made. Generally, time series data is often considered the dark horse of machine learning. There are many quirks of time series which make designing effective AI systems challenging. These challenges are extended to the development of interpretable AI which is lacking in research into interpretable time series methods.


Most recent progress in interpretable AI is focused on the latter stages of the machine learning pipeline such that explanations are offered to the end user. This stage of my PhD focuses on applying interpretable AI earlier in the machine learning pipeline, particularly focusing on techniques that will facilitate data analysis and feature engineering for time series data in health applications.

Explaining and Integrating Multimodal Data

Due to the complex and interconnected nature of biological systems, a model trained on a single data source will not be effective in predicting an accurate outcome . It is therefore crucial to combine multiple data sources to create holistic and effective predictive models. Machine learning that integrates multimodal data could be applied to decision-support-systems in clinical practice, federated learning across medical departments or to combine the output from various wearable devices. The explanation of these systems becomes more complex as data and machine learning components are combined in different ways. This project will use the output of the previous phase to develop explanations for integrated multi modal data

A key barrier to the widespread adoption of AI within medicine is the lack of clarity surrounding the communication between human clinician and AI application.
